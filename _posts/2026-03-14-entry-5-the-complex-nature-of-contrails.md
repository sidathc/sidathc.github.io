## Entry 5: The Complex Nature of Contrails

While looking for answers on the relationship between contrails and time of day, I stumbled across [this article](https://www.science.org/content/article/airplane-contrails-may-not-be-climate-villain-once-feared) from science.org.

The article discusses how contrails are considered to trap large amounts of heat (around 3x more than CO2) and how researchers have collaborated with airlines recently to avoid contrail formation by modifying flight routes to avoid certain altitudes based on weather data, like the trial with American Airlines seen on the [Google Contrails homepage](https://sites.research.google/gr/contrails/).

The article then talks about how recent studies have identified some limitations to this approach. To summarise some of the main points:

* Small-scale trials from 2021 showed some weather models only predict where contrails form 1/5th of the time due to the contrail field being "data-starved".

* One study found over 80% of contrails in their dataset formed in existing clouds than in clear skies. These could have a reduced warming effect, or even a cooling effect, which needs to be accounted for in contrail-avoidance schemes

* Another study found that contrail warming was not 3x that of CO2, but rather 2/3rd, suggesting a smaller impact that previously perceived

---

My main takeaway from this article is understanding how complicated contrail formation is and how much more knowledge is needed to better understand their climate impacts, carefully balancing fuel usage and the warming impacts from each contrail region. 

What this means for me: it motivates me to keep analysing data in Australia, an underexplored region of the world, and share these findings.
