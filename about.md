### What are contrails?

Contrails (or condensation trails) are those white lines you see in the sky when planes fly overhead. They form when hot, humid air from jet engine exhaust mixes with the extremely cold air at high altitudes, turning water vapour into ice crystals.

How long a contrail lingers depends on surrounding atmospheric conditions. In dry air it evaporates quickly; in humid air the ice particles grow, and the contrail can persist and spread for hours.

### Why contrails matter

Aircraft are responsible for around 2% of total global warming<sup>[[1]](#ref1)</sup> and the aviation industry continues to grow post-pandemic. CO2 emissions make up most of the exhaust, but contrails from water vapour make up a majority of non-CO2 emissions.

Contrails do this by trapping heat in the atmosphere — with some estimates putting their warming effect on par with or exceeding that of aviation's direct carbon emissions<sup>[[2]](#ref2)</sup>. As air traffic continues to grow, so does the scale of this effect. Recent research has complicated the picture further: warming estimates vary widely, and contrails forming inside existing cloud cover may behave very differently to those in clear skies. There is still a lot we don't fully understand.

### Why focus on Australia?

Most contrail research has focused on Europe and North America — regions with dense air traffic and well-studied atmospheres. Australia is comparatively underexplored, despite having a unique atmospheric environment and a heavy reliance on long-haul air travel due to its geographic isolation. Google's Project Contrails, for example, hopes to extend its models to places in Oceania in the future<sup>[[3]](#ref3)</sup>.

### About the author

My name is **Sidath Chandrasena**. I'm a software engineer with a dual degree in Integrative Engineering & International Studies and a minor in Data Science. This project sits at the intersection of my background in data and my long-standing interest in aviation and the environment. The analysis is built on Python, PostgreSQL, and GeoPandas, using data from the Google Contrails API and OpenSky Network.

Growing up in Australia, I saw first-hand that reliance on air travel. **Contrails Australia** is my personal project to examine contrail formation in Australian skies using real atmospheric data — identifying hotspots, understanding the conditions that create them, and exploring what avoidance might look like in practice.
