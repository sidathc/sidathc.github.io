<section class="sm-split">
  <div class="sm-split-text fade-in">
    <h2 class="sm-section-heading">Context</h2>
    <div class="sm-body">
      <p>Australia was first inhabited by First Nations peoples until 1788, when European settlement began under Captain Arthur Phillip.</p>
      <p>Migration to Australia was exclusive to Europeans under a series of policies implemented until the 1960s that sought to heavily restrict non-European migration.</p>
      <p>Yet over 500 non-Europeans from Ceylon — now Sri Lanka — arrived in 1882, and many remained in Australia after federation in 1901.</p>
      <p><strong>Why did this occur?</strong></p>
    </div>
  </div>

<!-- ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════
     BLACKBIRDING
     ══════════════════════════════════════════════════════ -->
<section class="sm-split sm-split-rev">
  <div class="sm-split-text fade-in">
    <h2 class="sm-section-heading">Blackbirding: Historical Context</h2>
    <div class="sm-body">
      <p>With the expansion of the sugar industry in North Queensland, planters faced a dilemma: harvesting sugarcane was considered 'degrading' work for locals, yet they did not wish for non-Europeans to potentially settle in Australia.</p>
      <p>As a solution, between 1863 and 1901, over 60,000 people from more than 80 Pacific Islands were brought — many through coercion and trickery — to work on Queensland plantations. This practice was known as <strong>'blackbirding'</strong>.</p>
      <p>The lack of laws or contracts to protect these workers meant that many died from the hazardous work and poor conditions.</p>
    </div>
  </div>
  <div class="sm-split-visual fade-in fade-in-delay-1">
    <img src="/assets/images/sri-lankan-migration/blackbirding.png" alt="South Sea Islanders planting cane in Queensland">
    <span class="sm-img-caption">Photo of South Sea Islanders planting cane · Source: Burdekin Shire Council Library Services</span>
  </div>
</section>

<!-- ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════
     DECISION TO HIRE
     ══════════════════════════════════════════════════════ -->
<section class="sm-split">
  <div class="sm-split-text fade-in">
    <h2 class="sm-section-heading">The Decision to Hire Labour from Ceylon</h2>
    <div class="sm-body">
      <p>The rising mortality rate and growing opposition from the Anti-Slavery Society in Britain made blackbirding unsustainable.</p>
      <p>Planters sought Indian Tamils who worked in British colonial plantations, but <em>'the British governors of India had no wish to see them treated in the same way as the Aboriginal or Kanaka (South Sea Islanders)'</em>.</p>
      <p>Planters looked to Ceylon. Bundaberg planter Horace Burkitt contacted Henry Caulfield, who resided in Ceylon, asking his assessment of Ceylonese workers.</p>
    </div>
  </div>
  <div class="sm-split-visual fade-in fade-in-delay-1">
    <img src="/assets/images/sri-lankan-migration/caulfield.png" alt="Henry St George Caulfield">
    <span class="sm-img-caption">Henry St George Caulfield, recruiter of the <em>Devonshire</em> migrants · From his autobiography, published 1937</span>
  </div>
</section>

<!-- ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════

<section class="sm-split sm-split-rev">
  <div class="sm-split-text fade-in">
    <h2 class="sm-section-heading">Factors that May Have Influenced Emigration from Ceylon</h2>
    <div class="sm-body">
      <p>Mass migration out of Ceylon was unprecedented in the 19th century. Scholars cite the following reasons for emigration:</p>
      <ul>
        <li><strong>Fall of the coffee industry</strong> in the 1870s and 1880s, leaving many workers without employment</li>
        <li><strong>Adventurousness</strong> — Caulfield noted migrants were <em>'all keen on seeing what the outside world was like'</em></li>
      </ul>
    </div>
  </div>
  <div class="sm-split-visual fade-in fade-in-delay-1">

</section>

<!-- ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════
     LEGISLATION
     ══════════════════════════════════════════════════════ -->
<section class="sm-split">
  <div class="sm-split-text fade-in">
    <h2 class="sm-section-heading">The Attempt to Legislate Emigration</h2>
    <div class="sm-body">
      <p>After Caulfield's advertisement was published in Ceylon, the colonial government grew concerned over the fairness of the contract offered to migrants.</p>
      <p>No emigration legislation had existed in Ceylon due to the lack of mass emigration. After correspondence with Queensland, it was determined that the contract was unfair.</p>
      <p>The Ceylon government began drafting legislation — but it was not proclaimed in time. The <em>Devonshire</em> departed Galle on <strong>15 October 1882</strong> with 500 people aboard.</p>
    </div>
  </div>

<!-- ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════
     ARRIVAL

<section class="sm-split sm-split-rev">
  <div class="sm-split-text fade-in">
    <h2 class="sm-section-heading">Arrival in Queensland &amp; Anti-Coolie League Disturbances</h2>
    <div class="sm-body">
      <p>The <em>Devonshire</em> left Point de Galle, Ceylon on <strong>15 October 1882</strong>, arriving in Mackay around <strong>10 November 1882</strong> and Bundaberg on <strong>17 November 1882</strong>.</p>
      <p>Fear and paranoia over 'coloured' labourers lowering work conditions resulted in the creation of <strong>'anti-coolie leagues'</strong> — organised groups that violently opposed the arrival of non-European workers.</p>
      <p>A violent confrontation between anti-coolie league members and the Sri Lankan migrants occurred upon arrival in Bundaberg, known as the <strong>'Battle of the Burnett'</strong>.</p>
    </div>
  </div>
  <div class="sm-split-visual fade-in fade-in-delay-1">
    <img src="/assets/images/sri-lankan-migration/arrival.png" alt="Point de Galle 1874 and the Burnett River Bundaberg 1909">
    <span class="sm-img-caption">Top: Point de Galle, 1874, by Sir Eliot Whately · Bottom: The Burnett River, Bundaberg, 1909 · Source: State Library of Queensland</span>
  </div>
</section>

<!-- ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════
     A FAILURE
     ══════════════════════════════════════════════════════ -->
<section class="sm-split">
  <div class="sm-split-text fade-in">
    <h2 class="sm-section-heading">A Failure</h2>
    <div class="sm-body">
      <p>The Battle of the Burnett incident became a propaganda victory for the anti-coolie league, as it publicised the planters' attempt to import foreign labour.</p>
      <p>The workers absconded service and went to jail instead, claiming they had been misled about working conditions in Queensland. Anti-coolie league members worsened the situation by inciting migrants to rebel against planters and preventing planters from reaching them.</p>
      <p>The migrants were British subjects and were entitled to rights. The planters, unwilling to take the matter to court, eventually let them go.</p>
      <p>Instead, the migrants drifted to other areas across Queensland and New South Wales in search of work — and many never left Australia.</p>
    </div>
  </div>
  <div class="sm-split-visual fade-in fade-in-delay-1">

</section>

<!-- ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════
     FINDINGS FROM LITERATURE
     ══════════════════════════════════════════════════════ -->
<section class="sm-split sm-split-rev">
  <div class="sm-split-text fade-in">
    <h2 class="sm-section-heading">Findings from Existing Literature</h2>
    <div class="sm-body">
      <p>Although Parts 1 &amp; 2 are well documented, little information exists about the fate of migrants after arriving in Australia. The findings are summarised below:</p>
      <p>Many workers <strong>broke their contracts</strong> and were no longer entitled to their return passage to Ceylon. Those who could afford the fare returned; a majority remained.</p>
      <p>Those who remained on the mainland <strong>struggled to find work</strong> due to the reluctance to hire non-Europeans. They intermarried with locals and assimilated into Australian society.</p>
      <p>Others moved to <strong>Thursday Island</strong>, where a distinct Sri Lankan community existed due to successive waves of migration from Asia.</p>
    </div>
  </div>

     METHODOLOGY
     ══════════════════════════════════════════════════════ -->
<section class="sm-split">
  <div class="sm-split-text fade-in">
    <h2 class="sm-section-heading">Methodology</h2>
    <div class="sm-body">
      <p>The <em>Devonshire</em> passenger list has been lost, and none of the graves of the migrants have been officially located.</p>
      <p>A dataset was constructed using the following sources:</p>
      <ul>
        <li>Ancestry.com — birth/death records &amp; electoral rolls</li>
        <li>Queensland State Archives</li>
        <li>Findagrave.com</li>
        <li>Index of Asiatic Aliens and Coloured Labourers, 1913</li>
      </ul>
      <p>Using cultural knowledge of Sri Lankan names — many of which were corrupted or confused with other non-European names over time — <strong>90 of the 500 migrants were identified</strong>.</p>
    </div>
  </div>

  <div class="sm-locations-inner">
    <h2 class="sm-section-heading">Summary &amp; Findings</h2>
    <div class="sm-body">
      <p>Of the 90 migrants identified, <strong>70 remained and died in Australia</strong>. A majority worked as farmers, labourers, cooks and gardeners. Groups of migrants appear to have clustered in the following locations, which warrant further research:</p>
    </div>
    <ul class="sm-location-list">
      <li>Homebush Plantation, Mackay, QLD</li>
      <li>Windermere Plantation, Bundaberg, QLD</li>
      <li>Goodnight Scrub, Gin Gin, QLD</li>
      <li>Dunwich Benevolent Asylum, QLD</li>
      <li>Thursday Island, QLD</li>
      <li>Chinderah &amp; Duranbah, NSW</li>
    </ul>
